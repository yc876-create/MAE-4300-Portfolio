Boeing 737 MAX Ethical Analysis Project

This portfolio summarizes my work from Weeks 6–9 in MAE 4300, where I analyzed the Boeing 737 MAX crashes through the lens of engineering ethics, regulation, and sociotechnical systems. Below is a brief overview of how my understanding developed each week.

Week 6 – Identifying Ethical Issues

I began by listing key ethical questions raised by the 737 MAX case, such as whether Boeing should have prioritized safety over schedule, been more transparent about MCAS, required redundant sensors, protected whistleblowers, and taken responsibility for victim compensation and reforms. This step framed the case as a series of concrete ethical dilemmas rather than just a technical failure.

Week 7 – Facts, Assumptions, and Definitions

I organized the case into material facts (e.g., MCAS relying on a single AOA sensor, limited pilot information), individual actions (decisions by engineers, managers, and FAA officials), and organizational factors (schedule pressure, weak safety culture, fragmented communication). I then filled in missing information with reasonable assumptions and clarified key terms like “safety,” “transparency,” and “accountability” from the perspectives of Boeing, regulators, and victims’ families.

Week 8 – Ethical Analysis and Resolutions

Using ASME/ASCE/ASEE codes, I mapped relevant principles to each ethical issue: protecting public safety, honesty in communication, avoiding conflicts of interest, and upholding professional responsibility. I argued that engineers should not have approved MCAS with a single point of failure, management should have invested in proper pilot training and disclosure, and Boeing should have supported a culture where safety concerns were taken seriously.

Week 9 – Constraints and Prevention Strategies

Finally, I examined real-world constraints that prevented ideal decisions, such as job security concerns, regulatory capture, market competition, and entrenched corporate culture. I then proposed prevention strategies at three levels:

  • Individual: stronger ethics training, documentation habits, and professional support networks.
  • Organizational: independent safety boards, clear authority for engineers to halt unsafe designs, and protection for whistleblowers.
  • Systemic: more independent regulatory oversight, stricter certification standards, and better alignment of incentives with long-term public safety.

Overall, this project taught me that engineering failures arise not only from flawed designs but from sociotechnical systems where ethics, regulation, incentives, and communication all interact. As a future engineer, I hope to carry forward the lesson that public safety and transparency must take precedence over schedule and profit.
