Cornell Animal Health Hackathon

Hackathon 3D Printing Presentation

The Cornell Animal Health Hackathon is the first Hackathon I have participated in! This Hackathon lasted 3 days and its purpose was to innovate a solution to a problem in the Animal Health Industry including pigmented animals, shortages in vet profession, specialty case back log, high volume testing, and more. I had originally participated in this event as it was suggested from my NBA 6650 class: Strategic Management of Technology, however this was one of my favorite experiences at Cornell.
Our team, Up Lift, focused on a solution to to assist in animal recovery without the physical demands of caretaking, address the shortage of veterinary staff, and explore ways to make it more accessible for consumers. Our idea was similar to that of a Hoyer lift that would support a dog's entire weight either in ACL recovery or post-anesthesia to provide range of motion and mobility for the dog if it wants to move, while also providing simplicity for caretakers to maneuver these animals without strain.
Though our idea did not move onto the final round, this experience taught me so much about the animal health by working with students in different dicsciplines, and how to work in a team to innovate a solution through brainstorming, prototyping, and pitching. I also gained a deeper appreciation for the design process of a product and tailoring it to a particular audiance and their needs!

RC Car for ECE 3140

RF Communication Bluetooth Microcontroller

My partner and I designed and programmed a Bluetooth-controlled RC car using an Arduino FRDM Board. Our design takes input from a Bluetooth controller (in our case a XBox Controller) and the controls are mapped on our laptop through Pygame. We then use the Arduino as a receiver between the computer and FRDM board where controls are mapped to GPIO functionality and PWM to our motor wheels. By using H-Bridges, we are able to go in multiple directions. See our website here GitHub Repo.

Sand Box Topographic Map for ECE 5725

Raspberry Pi Language: C Embedded Systems

Built an topographic sandbox with real-time topographic projection using an XBox 360 1414 Kinect, a Projector, and a Raspberry Pi. By getting depth values in the sand and filtering out values not in range, we normalize data points and perform a linear mapping of RBG colors. We made use of a Linux open source library, OpenKinect to abstract the values from the Kinect. This library also makes use of all four cores making use all all effective hardware, letting us run nearly in real-time. See our website here GitHub Repo.

8-Bit ALU in Cadence Verisium for ECE 4760

Cadence Virtuoso Routing VLSI

Designed an 8-bit arithmetic logic unit for VLSI using Cadence on the gate level and performed routing. We then verified the ALU worked through creating a testbench. By using knowledge we have learned in our Computer Archectiure and Microelectronics class, we interact with the boundary between digital hardware and circuitry. Using Cadence, we were able to explore the delays from our circuit and find to route and connect each hardware level component.
